Smartphone Predictive Pricing Analysis

regression
python
lasso
random-forest
feature-engineering
Built a multi-stage regression pipeline on 980 real-world smartphones — OLS baseline, Lasso feature selection, log-linear transformation, and Random Forest — to identify key pricing drivers and achieve R² = 0.847.
Author

Jinrui Zhang

Published

December 9, 2025

Problem

The global smartphone market generates billions in revenue, yet pricing decisions are surprisingly opaque. Which specs actually move the needle on price — and which are just marketing noise?

Our team tackled two questions:

  1. Which smartphone specifications most significantly influence price?
  2. Can we build an effective model to predict smartphone prices from published specs alone?

Data

  • Source: Kaggle — Real World Smartphones Dataset (Abhijit Dhatonde)
  • Size: 980 entries × 22 variables; 949 usable after cleaning
  • Coverage: 46 brands, 13 processor families, prices in Indian Rupees (INR)
  • Notable: Right-skewed price distribution (mean ≈ 75th percentile); max ₹650,000 (Vertu luxury brand)

Key variables: price, processor_speed, ram_capacity, internal_memory, battery_capacity, resolution_height/width, os (engineered → ios_not)

Approach

Stage 1 — EDA & Preprocessing

Price distribution was severely right-skewed (skewness = 12.68), signalling a log transformation would be needed. Correlation heatmap revealed a strong negative relationship between processor_speed and extended_memory_available — high-end phones drop SD card slots in favour of fast internal storage — flagging multicollinearity risk.

Cleaning decisions:

  • Median imputation for avg_rating, processor_speed, battery_capacity (40+ missing each)
  • Dropped fast_charging (211 missing), model (980 unique classes), processor_brand (13 classes) — high-cardinality or redundant
  • Engineered ios_not binary flag to capture Apple’s ecosystem premium

Stage 2 — OLS Baseline

Full-variable OLS revealed the problem: R² = 0.430, condition number = 1.76 × 10⁵ (severe multicollinearity). Multiple features had insignificant p-values. Regularization was needed.

Stage 3 — Lasso Feature Selection

LassoCV (10-fold, α ≈ 1759) compressed 15 features down to 8 meaningful predictors:

Feature Lasso Coeff Direction
internal_memory 11,988 positive
ios_not 5,538 positive
resolution_width 4,078 positive
resolution_height 2,607 positive
processor_speed 1,265 positive
extended_memory_available -6,069 negative
battery_capacity -1,971 negative
fast_charging_available -955 negative

Stage 4 — Linear Regression with Lasso Features

Re-fitting OLS on the 8 selected features: R² = 0.730. Residual analysis still showed heteroscedasticity — prediction errors fanned out at higher prices.

Stage 5 — Log-Linear Regression (Primary Model)

Applied log(price + 1) transformation. R² = 0.847 — explains ~85% of price variation. Residuals became approximately normal with heteroscedasticity largely resolved.

Key log-linear coefficients:

Feature Coefficient Interpretation
ios_not +0.584 iOS adds ~79% price premium over Android
processor_speed +0.454 Each GHz adds ~57% to price
extended_memory_available -0.279 SD card slot signals lower-tier device
internal_memory +0.002 Each GB adds ~0.2%

Stage 6 — Random Forest

To capture non-linear interactions (e.g., “small screen + iOS + high performance” = premium), trained Random Forest (100 trees) on Lasso-selected features: R² = 0.823.

Top feature importances: resolution_height (28%), processor_speed (25%), battery_capacity (17%). Notably ios_not dropped to 1.5% — because RF splits on hardware specs that already isolate the iPhone cluster, making OS redundant.

Model Comparison

Model R² Notes
OLS (all features) 0.430 Severe multicollinearity
LR + Lasso features 0.730 Clean feature set, still heteroscedastic
Log-Linear (primary) 0.847 Best interpretability, satisfies regression assumptions
Random Forest 0.823 Best for non-linear interactions, less interpretable

Key Findings

Performance drives price. Processor speed, internal memory, and screen resolution are the most consistent pricing factors across every model — fundamental to perceived product value.

Peripheral features add noise, not price. Fast charging, SD card support, and camera count show weak or negative relationships with price once performance specs are controlled.

The iOS premium is real and large. Even after fully controlling for hardware, iOS devices command a ~79% price premium — capturing Apple’s ecosystem, software longevity, and brand equity.

An interesting model contradiction: Log-linear attributes large influence to ios_not, while Random Forest nearly ignores it. RF first splits on resolution and processor speed, which effectively identifies the “high-end device” cluster (which happens to include most iPhones). Once that cluster is isolated, OS provides little additional information gain — the brand premium is absorbed into hardware splits.

Limitations

  • 980 observations is modest for ML — limits generalization to niche brands and new releases
  • Prices in INR, not USD — market conditions and tariffs limit cross-market applicability
  • No temporal dimension — snapshot data; cannot capture depreciation curves or launch cycles
  • Macroeconomic blind spots — supply chain costs, import tariffs, and regional purchasing power are not captured
