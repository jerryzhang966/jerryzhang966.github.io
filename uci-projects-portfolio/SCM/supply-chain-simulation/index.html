Overview

In UCI’s Supply Chain Analytics course, our team of four ran the Harvard Business Publishing Global Supply Chain Management Simulation V2 — a multi-round exercise where we acted as the supply chain leadership of a smartphone manufacturer, making interconnected decisions across product design, demand forecasting, and production sourcing over four simulated years.

Detail
Simulation HBP Global Supply Chain Management V2
Team Team 7 — 4 members
Models Model A (lower markdown cost) · Model B (high liquidation cost)
Decision areas Product design · Demand forecasting · Supplier selection · Production orders
Total profit $197,480,640 across 4 years

Simulation Structure

Each year consisted of three decision rooms:

Design Room — Choose up to 2 feature options (e.g., Upgraded Communication, Stylish, High Quality Audio). Each option shifts the expected demand distribution for Model A and/or B, affecting both the mean and variance of forecasts.

Forecast Room — Listen to 5 internal forecasters with different expertise levels. Combine their inputs to produce a single demand forecast for each model. Credibility weighting and variance between forecasters both matter.

Production Room — Allocate production across 4 suppliers with different cost/lead-time tradeoffs:

Supplier Cost Lead Time Best Use
FarFarAway Lowest 4 months Predictable, high-volume base
FarAway Moderate Medium Mid-flexibility buffer
PrettyClose Higher Short Reactive adjustments
VeryClose Highest Shortest Emergency only

A key asymmetry: Model B carries much higher holding, markdown, and liquidation costs than Model A — any overstock of Model B is disproportionately expensive.

Year-by-Year Strategy

Year 1 — Establishing a Baseline

Design: Upgraded Communication + Stylish. Rationale: align with the company’s “faster and sleeker” design brief. Stylish partially offset the demand variance introduced by Upgraded Communication.

Forecast: Selectively weighted — trusted Andrei (industry experience, Y) and Claire (specialist forecaster, Y) over less-credentialed forecasters. Model A: 59.5k, Model B: 35k.

Suppliers: FarFarAway (50k Model A, 10k Model B) + FarAway (10k Model B) + PrettyClose (reactive buffer for Model B). Issued one production change order mid-year after Model B demand increased, adjusting PrettyClose upward.

Result: $47,327,100 | Board votes: 2/5 Board criticism centered on the communication feature choice and limited supplier flexibility.

Year 2 — Shift Toward Predictability

Design: Stylish + Storage Capacity. Both options reduced standard deviation across both models. Avoided Extended Battery and Durability specifically because they introduced extreme demand variance — the priority shifted from demand mean to demand predictability.

Forecast: Used the average of all forecasters given low inter-forecaster variance. Model A: 55k, Model B: 32k.

Suppliers: Same structure as Year 1. Issued two production change orders after major demand events caused sharp drops — reduced FarFarAway and FarAway production to avoid excess Model B inventory.

Result: $51,572,640 | Board votes: 3/5 Improvement recognized in design option evaluation; feedback noted need for better sourcing flexibility.

Year 3 — Leaner and More Flexible

Design: High Quality Audio only. This was the only option that decreased variance for both models while also lifting Model B’s average expected demand. Rejected Extended Battery, Durability, and Anti-Theft — all increased demand uncertainty significantly.

Forecast: Model A: 67k (above average of 65; holding cost is low so slight overstock is acceptable). Model B: 26k (below average of 29; liquidation cost risk justified a conservative stance).

Suppliers: Dropped FarAway entirely — $2M setup cost, and over-reliance in Year 2 had reduced flexibility. Shifted to FarFarAway (60k Model A) + PrettyClose (26k Model B + 7k Model A buffer). Issued 2 change orders to reduce PrettyClose production as Model B demand fell.

Result: $44,326,300 | Board votes: 4/5 Lower profit due to ongoing demand volatility in Model B, but board recognized improved inventory balancing and design option reasoning.

Year 4 — Optimized and Disciplined

Design: High Quality Audio again. Achieved SD = 1 for Model A — near-perfect predictability. Only slight increase in Model B variance in exchange for higher average demand on both models.

Forecast: Model A: 63k (simple average, SD = 1 makes it reliable). Model B: 20k (cautious — historical demand consistently underperformed; average was 26k but SD = 9).

Suppliers: FarFarAway (60k Model A) + PrettyClose (20k Model B + 3k Model A). Did not issue production change orders this year.

Result: $54,254,600 | Board votes: 4/5 Highest single-year profit. Board recognized understanding of forecast credibility weighting and the asymmetric cost structures of Model A vs. B.

Key Lessons

Demand variance matters as much as demand level. Choosing design options that lower the standard deviation — even at the cost of a slightly lower expected mean — improved forecast accuracy and inventory outcomes more than chasing higher average demand with higher uncertainty.

Asymmetric inventory costs require asymmetric strategies. Model B’s high liquidation cost meant that the optimal forecast was below the mean (intentionally conservative), while Model A’s lower markdown cost allowed slight overstock. The same “average-based” rule would have been appropriate for one model and costly for the other.

Cheapest supplier ≠ best supplier. FarFarAway’s low cost was valuable for predictable Model A production, but its 4-month lead time made it unsuitable for the reactive Model B supply. FarAway was dropped in Year 3 because its $2M setup cost couldn’t be justified when PrettyClose provided equivalent flexibility at lower overhead.

Forecast credibility weighting outperforms consensus. Early rounds demonstrated that averaging all forecasters diluted signal from high-credibility sources. Weighting by track record and expertise — particularly Andrei’s industry experience — improved accuracy.

Production change orders are a tool, not a fallback. Issuing 2 change orders in Years 2 and 3 to reduce overexposed Model B inventory reflected proactive demand monitoring, not reactive panic. Year 4’s zero change orders reflected supply alignment with demand — a sign of improved forecasting, not passivity.
