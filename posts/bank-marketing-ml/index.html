Predicting Bank Marketing Campaign Subscriptions

machine-learning
classification
python
imbalanced-data
Compared Decision Tree and Logistic Regression classifiers on imbalanced UCI Bank Marketing data, addressed data leakage, applied SMOTE, and used GridSearchCV to identify high-probability customers for term deposit subscriptions.
Author

Jinrui Zhang

Published

December 1, 2025

Problem

A Portuguese bank ran telemarketing campaigns from 2008–2010, calling customers to promote term deposit subscriptions. Only ~11% of customers said yes — the vast majority declined. The business problem: how do you predict before picking up the phone which customers are actually worth calling?

Wasted calls mean wasted time, labor, and budget. A good model translates directly into campaign ROI.

Data

  • Source: UCI Bank Marketing Dataset — Portuguese bank, 41,188 records, May 2008–November 2010
  • Size after preprocessing: 43,193 rows × 13 features
  • Target: y — did the customer subscribe to a term deposit? (binary; ~11% positive — severe class imbalance)
Feature Group Variables
Demographics age, job (employed/retired/unemployed), marital status, education level
Financial avg. bank balance, housing loan, personal loan, credit default
Campaign call duration, # contacts this campaign, days since last contact, # previous contacts

Approach

Tools: Python · scikit-learn · imbalanced-learn · pandas · matplotlib · seaborn (Google Colab)

Preprocessing

  • Removed rows with “unknown” entries in job, marital, education
  • Simplified 12 job categories → 3: employed / retired / unemployed
  • Dropped low-signal columns: contact type, day, month, previous outcome
  • One-hot encoded categorical variables; converted yes/no → 1/0
  • Removed duration (call length only known after the call — a leakage feature)

Models

  • Decision Tree Classifier — captures non-linear relationships, produces interpretable splits
  • Logistic Regression — linear baseline with interpretable coefficients

Experiments

Each model was evaluated across four pipeline stages:

Stage Description
Benchmark 1 Numerical features only, minimal preprocessing
Benchmark 2 Full cleaning: binning, encoding, column removal
SMOTE Synthetic oversampling to balance the 11/89 class split
SMOTE + GridSearchCV Hyperparameter tuning to maximize Class 1 recall

Primary metric: F1-score (appropriate for imbalanced binary classification). All models evaluated with 10-fold cross-validation.

Findings

Model Comparison (Test F1 Score)

Model CV F1 Test F1 Notes
Untuned Decision Tree 0.392 0.403 Baseline
Untuned Logistic Regression 0.307 0.316 Baseline
Decision Tree (no duration) 0.242 0.244 Realistic pre-call scenario
Logistic Regression (no duration) 0.018 0.023 Collapsed without leakage
Decision Tree + SMOTE 0.837 0.228 Severe overfitting
Logistic Regression + SMOTE 0.785 0.244 Moderate gain
Tuned Decision Tree 0.471 0.470 Best generalization
Tuned Logistic Regression 0.350 0.330 Modest improvement

Key Takeaways

Data leakage was the biggest trap. duration (call length) was the strongest predictor across all models — but it is only known after the call ends, making it useless for pre-call targeting. Removing it cut F1 from ~0.40 to ~0.24, revealing the true predictive ceiling of the remaining features.

SMOTE overfit badly. Synthetic oversampling pushed cross-validation F1 to 0.84 but collapsed on the test set (0.23). Synthetic minority samples did not generalize to real customer behavior — a cautionary result about evaluating CV scores on SMOTE-resampled training data.

Decision Tree consistently outperformed Logistic Regression on F1 metrics. Non-linear decision boundaries matter here; subscription behavior does not separate cleanly on a hyperplane.

Best model: Tuned Decision Tree (max_depth=5, class_weight='balanced', min_samples_split=10) — Test F1 of 0.47, with precision 0.33 and recall 0.81. High recall means most real subscribers are captured; lower precision means some non-subscribers are contacted, which is the acceptable trade-off in a marketing context.

Top Predictors (after removing duration)

  1. Campaign contact frequency (more contacts → lower subscription probability)
  2. Housing loan status
  3. Previous campaign outcomes
  4. Job type (retired and unemployed had higher subscription rates)
  5. Education level (tertiary education correlated with higher subscription)

Business Recommendations

  1. Cap contacts at 1–2 per customer. Repeated calling reliably reduces subscription probability. Stop after two attempts.
  2. Prioritize high-signal segments: retired customers, higher education, no personal loans, positive prior campaign history.
  3. Tailor messaging by segment: retired customers respond to security and stability; highly-educated customers respond to long-term growth projections.
  4. Integrate the tuned Decision Tree as a lead scorer. The interpretable split rules can be embedded directly in a campaign management workflow to rank leads before dialing.

Reflections

The leakage discovery was the most important moment of this project. A model with duration looks strong (F1 ~0.40 baseline); without it, the real ceiling becomes clear and the work becomes honest. In a real deployment, this distinction determines whether the model is actually useful.

SMOTE served as a useful negative experiment. The proper approach is to apply SMOTE inside cross-validation folds using a Pipeline, not before splitting — that architectural mistake explained the wide CV/test divergence.

What I would do differently:

  • Use imblearn.pipeline.Pipeline to apply SMOTE inside CV folds properly
  • Test ensemble methods — Random Forest and XGBoost handle class imbalance more robustly than a single Decision Tree
  • Use precision-recall curves instead of a fixed 0.5 threshold, letting business requirements set the operating point
